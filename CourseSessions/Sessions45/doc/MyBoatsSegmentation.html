Business Decisions

In the second part of the case, our team has conducted segmentation and profiling of customers using clustering analysis. We identified 6 distinct segments, each with their own characteristics and preferences. For example, segment 3 are mostly young single men who are passionate about boats and hence care about thier power, style and brand. They also have quite a bit of disponsible income since are mostly single. Another group, segment 5, consists of older and wealthier individuals who are very dissatisfied with brand of their current boat. Using these profiles we can also derive some insights about which group should be target of company's marketing efforts. In our opinion, segments 1 and 4 constitute good targets and possibly segment 5. Segment 3 looks very appealing, but may be difficult to convince to switch brands.

For the second part of the analysis, we use the whole dataset from the file Boats.csv that contains responses to survey about boats by 2813 boat owners. In addition to the first 29 questions about individuals' attitudes, needs and motivations related to boats, the survery has 15 more questions which we will use to cluster and profile the respondends.

Our Approach

Clustering and Segmentation in 9 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Decide which variables to use for clustering

  4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

  5. Visualize Individual Attributes and Pair-wise Distances between the Observations

  6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

  7. Profile and interpret the clusters

  8. Assess the robustness of our clusters

Let's follow these steps.

Step 4. Define similarity or dissimilarity measures between observations

In our case we explore two distance metrics: the commonly used Euclidean distance as well as a simple one we define manually.

The Euclidean distance between two observations (in our case, customers) is simply the square root of the average of the square difference between the attributes of the two observations (in our case, customers). Let's now define our own distance metric, as an example. Let's say that the management team of the company believes that two customers are similar if they do not differ in their ratings of the attitudinal questions by more than 2 points. We can manually assign a distance of 1 for every question for which two customers gave an answer that differs by more than 2 points, and 0 otherwise. It is easy to write this distance function in R:

My_Distance_function<-function(x,y){sum(abs(x-y)>2)}

Step 6. Select the clustering method to use and decide how many clusters to have

After some experimentation, we chose to go with the Hierarchical method, which allowed us to split the respondends into 6 groups. We arrived at this number of clusters by gradually increasing the number of clusters from 2 and observing on dendogram how much the distance between the clusters increased by adding one more cluster. There is very little change in distance between setup with 6 vs. 7 clusters.

In this note we will use two widely used methods: the Kmeans Clustering Method, and the Hierarchical Clustering Method. Like all clustering methods, these two also require that we have decided how to measure the distance/similarity between our observations. Explaining how these methods work is beyond our scope. The only difference to highlight is that Kmeans requires the user to define how many segments to create, while Hierarchical Clustering does not.

Let's fist use the Hierarchial Clustering method, as we do not know for now how many segments there are in our data. Hierarchical clustering is a method that also helps us visualise how the data may be clustering together. It generates a plot called the Dendrogram which is often helpful for visualization - but should be used with care. For example, in this case the dendrogram, using the euclidean distance metric from the earlier steps and the ward hierarchical clustering option (see below as well as help(hclust) in R for more information), is as follows:

Note that we can draw as many clusters are we choose (e.g. in this case we chose 6 clusters) around the branches of the Dendrogram.

We can also plot the “distances” traveled before we need to merge any of the lower and smaller in size clusters into larger ones - the heights of the tree branches that link the clusters as we traverse the tree from its leaves to its root. If we have n observations, this plot has n-1 numbers.

Step 7. Profile and interpret the clusters

From the tabe below, we can see that the values for the 6 segments differ slightly, but not too much. Interestingly, it looks like on average respondents own less than one boat and would quite likely purchase one in future. Segment 3 seems to be most likely to buy and segment 6 is least likely to buy.Comparing other key demographics of each segment to population average, we can also say that segment 3 consists of mostly young males, while Segment 6 is older and has many more females. In line with expectations, segment 3 is also less wealthy than population average, while segment 6 has substantially higher income.

In our case, assuming we decided we use the 6 segments found using Kmeans as outlined above (similar profiling can be done with the results of hierarchical clustering or other segmentation methods), we can see how the responses to our survey differ across segments. Looking at the heatmap of responses below, we can try to describe the 6 segments we clustered our respondents into. These segments can potentially be useful to identify groups for targeted marketing. The segments can be described as follows: Segment 1: This group consists mostly of young employed males, who care quite a bit about brand but are not too happy with their current boat and would not buy that brand again. Segment 2: This group is interesting and yet difficult to describe at the same time, because its characteristica are not leaning in any direction. Thus, this group could be called “Average Joe.” Segment 3: In contrast, this group stands out in many dimensions, especially in terms of sattisfaction from having the best boat in its class. These people (mostly young males with less income due to only staring their professional life) care very much about issues such as brand and horsepower and superior look of the boats. This group could perhaps be compared to young men who are passionate about their cars and tend to tune them up. Not surprisingly, this group is very happy with the boat they own and would most likely not switch to another brand. Segment 4: This group can be in short described as “boat wanna-owner” because many of the members don't own the boat yet but would definitelly like to have one, even if the boat is not the best in its class. Perhaps the reason is they many of them are not working and don't have necessary income to make the purchase. Segment 5: This group consists of older, wealthier individuals who own a boat but don't use it too often because they are extremely dissatisfied with the brand and would not recommend it. Segment 6: This group consist of the wealthiest individuals in the sample, but also the oldest. These people are defnitelly not single and quite a few are women. They don't have high standards regarding boats and don't need to impress anyone. They seem to belong to a camp of “traditionalists” - people have boats but don't expect much from them. They also seem not to have clear views about the brand of boat they own because of extreme responses to questions about recommendaton and eventual future purchase of the same brand of boat.

Intepretation, Decisions and Conclusion

From the clustering analysis above, we can derive some conclusions that can have business applications going beyond pure description of the segments. Specifically, we can identify those segments which are most likely to buy a different boat and where the boat company should channel its marketing efforts. In this context, segments 1 and 4 seem like good candiadates, because they either are not happy with their current brand of boats but care about brand and boat's specs and have lots of disposable income, or don't have a boat yet but really would like to buy one in as soon as they can afford. Another potential fertile ground is segment 5, because these are wealthy individuals who are very unhappy with their current boat. This segment can offer lots of opportunities providing the boat company through adveritsing convinces these people use boats more often. On the other hand, inasmuchas optimistic the segment 3 looks like at first sight, targeting them would be a tough sell because they are very loyal to their brand. If, however, it would be possible to convince them to switch, the company can lock in great customers.
