Boats Segmentation: Factor Analysis

Team members: Neha Molugu, Rene Willemars and Bartosz Augustyniak

Business Decisions

Having analyzed the data, we decided that eight distinct factors were enough to describe the data contained in the responses to the first 29 questions of the Boats case. These eight factors can be broadly described as: 1) Brand/price, 2) Experience driven, 3) Functionality driven , 4) Power driven, 5) Boating enthusiast, 6) Boat geek, 7) Water sports and 8) Passion driven

The Data

We were using the dataset of responses provided by 2813 individuals (boat owners) to the questionnaire about boats. For this excrcise, we chose to use only responses to the first 29 questions, which refer to attitudes, motivations and needs of the boat owners. We verified that all data are numeric in nature and are metric. In loading the dataset, we omitted the first column containing numerical identifier of a respondent because it is not needed for this excericse and large values for identifier unnecessarily complicated presentation of the data.

Our Approach

Factor Analysis in 6 steps

  1. Confirm the data in metric

  2. Decide whether to scale or standardize the data

  3. Check the correlation matrix to see if Factor Analysis makes sense

  4. Develop a scree plot and decide on the number of factors to be derived

  5. Interpret the factors (consider factor rotations - technical but useful)

  6. Save factor scores for subsequent analyses

Step 3: Check correlation matrix to see if Factor Analysis makes sense

Looking at the correlation matrix below, one can see that Factor Analysis might make sense. Although correlations between individual questions are not as strong as in the MBAs example, most of correlations seem to fall between values of 0.3 and 0.44. Also, quite a few questions have correlations 0.5 or above. This suggest that these questions could be grouped under a few common factor base without losing too much of the information contained in them.

Step 4. Develop a scree plot and decide on the number of factors to be derived

The table below shows the variance explained (we kept the default 65% for minimum variance explained). Using the eigenvalues as citerion, we should select only 5 factors to explain the dataset. However, these 5 factors cumulatively explain only just over half of the variation in the data. We believe this is too little and hence decided to increase the number of factors to 6, which cumulatively explain almost 56 percent of the variation. Also, even the 6th factor has eingenvalue of 0.9, which means it is almost as valuable as any individual question.




Looking at scree plot only confirms our earlier selection. The “elbow” of the curve is either at factor 2, which is defintlly too little, or at factor 6.

5. Interpret the factors

For our data, the 5 selected factors look as follows after the varimax rotation:



To better visualize and interpret the factors we often “supress” loadings with small values, e.g. with absolute values smaller than 0.5. In this case our factors look as follows after suppressing the small numbers:



How would you interpret the selected factors?

We used the 7 factors as the starting point to see if we obtain a more coherent loading between the factors and questions that can be naturally groupped together by changing the number of factors. Because of our experiments, we realized that actually 8 factors would make more sense, which cumulatively explain 61 percent of variation. These factors can be characterized as follows: 1) Value for money (brand/price), 2) Boating as an exciting experience (experience driven), 3) Boat as functional device (functionality driven) , 4) Boat as expression of strong personality (power driven), 5) Boating enthusiast, 6) Boat geek, 7) Boat as water sports gear and 8) Hardcore boat owner (passion driven)
